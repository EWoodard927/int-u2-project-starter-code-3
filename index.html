The Service Dog's Struggle

Whether in the largest cities or the smallest towns, many service dog owners have faced discrimination, often in the form of being rejected from public spaces such as grocery stores, restaurants, or malls. Unawareness of a service dog owner's rights only reinforces these actions. Service dogs and their owners are allowed access to the majority of public spaces, including buildings, with the exception of areas that may be dangerous, such as factory floors. What's more, these service dogs have life-saving jobs to perform. Medical alert dogs are often used to detect oncoming heart issues, POTS attacks, or seizures. Pressuring service dog owners to go without these dogs could result in a mishandled medical issue that could threaten their life.

Debunking Common Myths About Service Dogs

Myth: Service dogs are limited to labrador retrievers and golden retrievers.

Fact: Service dogs can be any breed! While labs and goldies are commonly trained as service animals, any breed of dog can successfully undergo the same training.


Myth: Service dog owners must carry the service dog's certification to enter a building.

Fact: There is no such thing as a service dog's certification, so owners certainly do not have to carry it.


Myth: Service dogs will always wear their service dog vests while out in public.

Fact: Service dogs are not required to wear a vest at any time. Many owners prefer to put the vest on their dog to avoid confusion, but not every owner prefers this.



Myth: Emotional support dogs are equivalent to service dogs and are also allowed access to buildings where pets are not usually permitted.

Fact: Emotional support dogs are not trained in the same manner as service dogs, and therefore are not guaranteed to behave in such spaces. They are not under the protection of the ADA, and are therefore not permitted to enter stores, as their presence could disrupt working service dogs. This is not to say that emotional support dogs are unimportant! They still perform life-changing roles that their owners are grateful for.

Our Job

As a service dog owner, entering no-pet zones and facing discrimination can be stressful. We want to make sure that service dog owners are comfortable navigating public spaces while knowing their rights. That's why the City Service Dog Initiative has partnered with business owners all over the tri-state area to teach about service dog accessibility. With our seminars, we teach the public about how to respect working service dogs. It's our hope that these efforts will make the tri-state area a friendlier and more accessible place for service dogs!
